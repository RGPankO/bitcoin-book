### 🔴 GAP #1: Time Preference — The Missing Core Concept

**What it is:** Austrian economics concept from The Bitcoin Standard Ch6-7. High time preference = live for now, discount the future. Low time preference = sacrifice present for future. Fiat money forces high time preference on everyone (inflation punishes saving → why save?). Sound money restores low time preference.

**Why it's not just a book concept — it's the deepest "why Bitcoin" answer:**
- Why do people spend instead of save? Because saving in fiat is losing.
- Why is modern architecture ugly? (Ammous: high time preference; people want short-term gains, not cathedrals built for centuries.)
- Why are birth rates falling across developed world? High time preference reduces investment in future.
- Why is corporate short-termism rampant? Quarterly earnings vs long-term compounding.
- Bitcoin forces low time preference: if your money appreciates, you think harder before spending it. You invest in the future.

**Bulgarian angle:** Bulgaria has one of Europe's lowest savings rates. High credit card debt. Short-term consumption culture that emerged post-1997 hyperinflation — after people lost everything in savings, why save? Time preference concept explains this perfectly and connects directly to the BG trauma already in Ch2.

**Currently in the book:** NOT AT ALL. Ch3 covers inflation and its damage. Ch11 covers why savings in leva are bad. But neither explains the *behavioral mechanism* — why inflated money changes how people think and act across generations.

**Recommendation:** NEW SECTION in Ch3 (Inflation chapter), approximately 1,500-2,000 words. Title: "Как инфлацията промени начина, по който мислим." Cover: time preference definition with concrete examples (cathedral builders vs modern shopping mall architects; grandparents who saved for 30 years vs generation that bought iPhones on credit). Connect to Bitcoin: sound money rewards low time preference, which historically built civilizations.

**Priority: HIGH — add to Ch3**
**Estimated additional words: 1,500**
**Related chapters: Ch3, Ch11, Ch18**

### 🔴 GAP #2: Bitcoin Makes War Expensive (The Fiat Standard / Bitcoin Standard Argument)

**What it is:** Ammous's argument in both books: fiat money enabled 20th century wars. Before WWI on gold standard, governments had to raise taxes to fund wars — a politically unpopular act that limited war duration. After going off gold, governments could print money to fund wars indefinitely. WWI was supposed to last months; it lasted 4 years. The financial mechanism changed the scale of human destruction.

Bitcoin as sound money: if you can't print money, you can't finance unlimited warfare. Wars would have to be funded by direct taxation, which faces political resistance. Sound money doesn't guarantee peace but it raises the cost of sustained conflict.

**Why it matters for a Bulgarian reader:**
- Bulgaria has lived under both Ottoman occupation and Soviet occupation. The monetary mechanism behind state power is not abstract here.
- Current Russia-Ukraine war financed in part by money printing — directly relevant, current.
- NATO expansion, European defense spending — all funded how? Printing.
- This is the *most powerful* political argument for Bitcoin that isn't just about personal finance.

**Currently in the book:** NOT PRESENT. Ch2 covers fiat creation and QE. Ch4 covers bail-outs. Neither makes the war-financing connection explicitly.

**Plamen's stance:** He's anti-government, anti-war-financing, skeptical of NATO/state power. This argument would be authentically his. He'd say: "Нали, ако не можеш да печаташ пари, не можеш да финансираш безкрайни войни. Биткойн не ни спасява от конфликти, но поне им слага таван."

**Important caveat:** This is a politically sensitive argument. Plamen should decide how far to go — from "sound money limits war financing" (defensible historical claim) to "Bitcoin would have prevented WWII" (overreach). Recommend the first framing.

**Recommendation:** NEW SECTION in Ch2 (Fiat Experiment), approximately 1,000-1,500 words. After covering QE and Cantillon effect, add section: "Как фиатните пари финансираха 20-ти век." Cover: WWI financing change when gold standard abandoned, scale of modern wars vs pre-fiat conflicts, Ukraine/Russia current example, the connection: inflation is not just economic — it's a war enabler.

**Priority: HIGH — add to Ch2**
**Estimated additional words: 1,200**
**Related chapters: Ch2, Ch4, Ch18**

**What's missing:**
- Bukele's background: young president, FMLN → Nuevas Ideas, anti-establishment populist

**Recommendation:** Expand Ch10 OR create a dedicated "Case Study: El Salvador" box within Ch12. Approximately 1,500 additional words. Keep it honest — include the failures (Chivo wallet problems, volcano bonds stalling) alongside the wins. Plamen is not a cheerleader, and presenting only successes would undermine credibility.

**Priority: MEDIUM — expand Ch10 or Ch12**
**Estimated additional words: 1,500**

### 🟡 EXPAND #2: Bitcoin vs CBDCs — Needs Elevation

**Current coverage:** One section in Ch18 ("CBDCs: обратното на Bitcoin"), approximately 300 words. Too brief for a topic this important.

**Why it needs more:** 
- Digital euro is actively in development; EU has committed to it. Bulgaria will adopt euro (likely 2025-2026). This is NOT hypothetical for Bulgarian readers — it's coming.
- CBDC is the most sophisticated counter-argument to Bitcoin that governments will deploy: "We have digital money too, safer and regulated."
- The programmable money argument (spending conditions, expiry dates, frozen accounts) needs proper evidence, not just assertion. China's digital yuan already has expiry dates on stimulus payments. This is documented.
- The "freedom money vs surveillance money" framing is excellent but needs concrete examples to be persuasive.

**Recommendation:** Expand CBDCs section in Ch18 from 300 to ~800-1,000 words. Add: China digital yuan real examples (expiry dates, geographic restrictions), EU digital euro current status and planned features, ECB statements on programmability, Agustín Carstens (Bank for International Settlements) quote about CBDCs giving central banks "absolute control." Then contrast with Bitcoin: no programmability by any central authority.

**Priority: MEDIUM — expand Ch18**
**Estimated additional words: 600**

### 🟡 EXPAND #3: Bitcoin Mining — Positive Story (Not Just Myth-Busting)

**Current coverage:** Ch17 myth #3 ("Биткойн консумира твърде много енергия") is a defensive rebuttal. No positive story about what mining actually does.

**What's missing:** The positive case is actually quite compelling:
- Stranded energy: miners go where energy would otherwise be wasted. Flared gas from oil fields (North Dakota, Permian Basin) that would otherwise just burn into atmosphere — miners capture it and monetize it. Net carbon reduction.
- Hydro overflow: in rainy season, hydroelectric dams produce more electricity than the grid can absorb. It literally dumps into the earth. Bitcoin miners buy this energy. Net: money for dam operators, no carbon.
- Grid stabilization: Bitcoin miners are ideal interruptible loads. Texas grid operator ERCOT has Bitcoin miners as emergency demand-response resources. When grid is stressed, miners shut off in seconds, freeing capacity.
- Methane capture: landfill and agricultural methane (far more potent greenhouse gas than CO2) burned by generators powering Bitcoin miners. Net: converts CH4 to CO2, which is ~80x less harmful over 20 years.
- The energy argument is not defensive — it's offensive.

**Recommendation:** Add 800-1,000 words to Ch17 myth #3, or create a positive section in Ch5 (origin chapter) or Ch6 (what is Bitcoin). The myth-rebuttal format is fine for Ch17, but a positive framing somewhere else would be valuable. 

**Priority: MEDIUM — expand Ch17 myth #3**
**Estimated additional words: 800**

## Bulgarian-Specific Gaps Assessment

One gap cuts across everything above: **the book doesn't sufficiently connect Bitcoin to the future of Bulgaria specifically.**

Ch2 uses BG 1996-97 excellently. Ch3 uses the croissant and bread story perfectly. Ch4 mentions IDS Bank. But then from Ch5 onward, Bulgaria mostly disappears and the book becomes generic global.

Specific missing Bulgarian angles:
- **Lev → Euro transition (likely 2025-2026):** Bulgarian readers are about to give up the lev they've lived with for 30 years. What does that mean for monetary sovereignty? CBDC risk becomes real at that moment.
- **Bulgarian pension system specifically:** Plamen mentions pensions as Ponzi generally. Bulgarian readers know the НОИ (National Social Security Institute). Name it. Call it what it is.
- **Bulgarian banks specifically:** OBB, UniCredit Bulbank, DSK — mentioned briefly in Ch18. Should appear in Ch4 (Banks chapter) when discussing fractional reserve. "Твоята сметка в OBB — прочети договора. Четяно ли е? Не. Провери кой е собственик на депозита ти."
- **Bulgarian tax treatment of Bitcoin gains:** Ch19 ("Какво да правиш ТИ") currently has "BG tax basics." Ensure this is accurate and specific — capital gains tax rate in Bulgaria, when it applies, how crypto is classified by NRA (Национална агенция за приходите).

This isn't a new chapter — it's a recurring reminder to Shadow when writing or revising to add Bulgarian specificity wherever the current text is generic.
